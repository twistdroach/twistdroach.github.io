Vintage Computing

The first computer that was available to me was an Apple IIe my parents bought used in the early 90's. It was on this device that I learned basic and machine language programming. I never had an assembler in the "old" days, so any assembly I wrote back then had to be hand assembled. I later acquired some old IBM compatible machines that my father's company were throwing out. I began using MS-DOS & Windows 3.1, but didn't have access to much in the way of dev tools (I missed the Borland C++ era). It was around this time that I downloaded a few disk images for muLinux, though I didn't have a machine capable of running it. I was able to boot it up (non-destructively) on my dad's 486 laptop from work. This was my first taste of linux, but I wasn't able to do much with it at the time (and I don't think I was successfull in downloading the compiler packages - a single 1.4M disk image could take multiple days to download back then).

A few years later, I visited my uncle who worked at a University. He gave me logins to their *nix systems, which was great as at the time my highschool was lackadasical about their computer security, so I could ssh (probably telnet back then - Gasp!) from our school computers via the internet. I spent most of my free time in study halls playing with these machines (these were the days of using pine as a mail client in your terminal). It was an ecclectic group of machines from what I remember, having at least a Sun SparcStation & NeXT workstation in the mix.

In college I worked as a sys admin for the ECE department. This was just as all of the proprietary unices were dieing out & being replaced by Linux. We had Solaris & Digital Unix systems, as well as a swath of RedHat Linux systems (before it was RHEL). Dumpster diving was great during this era - I found DEC, HP-UX, & Sun Systems, sometimes complete with install media. I still have some of them today.

This is a not quite exhaustive catalog of the systems I have collected over the years. There is roughly three eras here, the pre-historic 80's systems, the *nix workstations of the late 80's through the 90's, and some rackmount server hardware from the 2000's.
